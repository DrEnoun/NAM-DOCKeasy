The big idea
Predict where and how strongly a small molecule binds — before you touch a wet bench.
Docking takes a receptor structure and a candidate ligand, then samples millions of orientations to find the geometry that minimises binding energy.

The output is a pose (a 3D arrangement) and a score (kcal/mol). Both inform what to test next — mutations, analogues, MD, assays.
  • 1
    Input. One cleaned receptor structure (PDB) + one prepared ligand (PDBQT).
  • 2
    Search. A scoring function ranks sampled poses by predicted affinity.
  • 3
    Output. Top poses with energies and interaction lists.
  • 4
    Validation. Re-dock the co-crystal ligand; confirm RMSD < 2.0 Å.

When to reach for it
Docking is for structure-based hypothesis generation.
✓
Good fit
Known receptor structure. Small-molecule ligand. You want to know where it binds and rank a few candidates.
~
Borderline
Homology model only. Large flexible receptor. Peptide ligand > 5 residues. Proceed with caution and ensemble docking.
✗
Skip if
No structure available. Protein–protein system (use HADDOCK instead). You need quantitative ΔG — that's MD or experiment.

Decide before docking
Which workflow applies to your study?

Is your binding site known?

→
Yes. Targeted docking — small grid around the residue cluster.
→
No. Blind docking first; refine with a focused run after.

How flexible is your receptor?

→
Rigid. AutoDock Vina is sufficient.
→
Side-chain flex. AD4 with flexible residues, or Vina-flex.
→
Large conformational change. Ensemble docking against MD frames.
